Greetings


     Germany is a country that uses formal greetings. It is useful to know the difference between the two. An informal greeting would be simply saying 'hi,' 'bye,' or 'see you' to a person, and leaving it at that. A formal greeting is considered something such as saying 'Hello Mr./Ms./Mrs. Smith,' or It was nice meeting you today Mr./Ms./Mrs. Smith.' Proper introductions are also included within this formal greeting. An example of a proper introduction would be 'Mr./Ms./Mrs. Smith, I would like to introduce you to John Doe.' Please notice that these formal greetings include titles and surnames, are you should continue to use these until given the proper cues from the person to use their first name.



Dining


     Once you are past meeting new people, you may run into a time where you are invited over. If you do end up meeting your new acquaintances for a meal, the following information may be of use to you. First and foremost, Germans are very punctual people; never arrive early to an event with a set time, and rather show up within the first fifteen minutes or so of the given time. If you do end up thinking that you may be late, do call ahead and explain your absence.

     Expanding on the formalities of etiquette please note that, depending on your closeness to the people you are visiting, you may want to remain standing until given a social cue to sit or if you are seated by the hosts. Keep in mind typical European table manners, as well. Table setup for a formal dinner will typically be Continental or European style, which means the fork will be on the left side and the knife will be on the right. You are to use them as such, and it is considered improper to swap back and forth with the utensils.



Quiet Hours

     By law, there are quiet hours in Germany. During the days of Monday through Saturday, between the hours of 10PM to 7AM, then from 1PM to 3PM no loud noises of any kind are to be tolerated or allowed. On Sundays and certain holidays this quiet time is to last all day. This includes, but isn't limited to, children running around playing loudly outside, loud radios, televisions, or music. Lawn care tools are also included within this, as well. Also note that any parties that may be hosted by a person, if it is to last into these quiet hour periods, should let neighbors know a few days ahead of time by either letter or in person. Even with a warning, neighbors can, if they wish, complain to authority about any loud noises going on around them.

Relationships


     Germany is a country that separates business and personal lives quite well. When it comes to making a business partnership or deal, you do not need to have a long and outstanding personal relationship with the person you are doing business with. The person will, rather, look at your previous credentials and work, as well as your company's history and make their opinions or decisions from there. Much like standard social greetings, business communications are also formal and should be completely professional. Exaggerations and generally over-hyped deals will not get you very far, as Germans tend to be extremely direct and blunt.



Meetings

     Meetings are typically formal and being punctual is key. Creating appointments in advance is usually mandatory, and may need to be posted a few days in advance. As with any other business meeting, being on time is extremely important. If you are to be delayed getting to the meeting for any reason, phone or message ahead to let the group know and to apologize. Meetings are to stick to a schedule and agenda, and you should include both start and end times to give the meeting goers a rough sense of how long the meeting will last. Note that initial meetings may be used simply to get to know each other as potential business partners. When entering a meeting being hosted by a local/German business, please remember that there is a very strict formality for entering the meeting room. The higher ranking business people will enter before all others, from highest to lowest rank within the company, men leading in before women.



Negotiations

     Business negotiations with Germans can be a bit tricky, but do have a strict set of protocols for the most part. When first entering the negotiation room, do not sit until you are absolutely told to. If told to sit in a specific seat, do so. As with meetings, a negotiation will have an absolutely predetermined agenda with both start and end times, as well as what will be negotiated. While not an absolute, most German negotiations will be very light on small talk and straight to business, as they want to stick to the schedule. Both you and your company's credentials, certifications, and history will be looked into in regards for the negotiation or contract. If there are any questions on the contract being signed, expect to go through every last detail until everyone is satisfied. Patience for this particular part is key, as Germans are very detail oriented. Note that any decision making on the German side of a business will be done in a hierarchical way, or in other words, the higher up company members are the decision makers.
